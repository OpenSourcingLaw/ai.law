## Information Privacy Law
Machine learning technologies, central to AI, require data inputs to determine outcomes. To the extent that those data inputs may contain information pertaining to individuals information privacy laws apply to their use.

### Origins
Australia’s _Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)_ (_"the Act"_), is concerned largely with information privacy law and the origins of this variety of privacy law can be traced to developments in International Law and domestic legislation in North America and Europe. Privacy is most readily raised in defence of invasion of it. Out of the persecution and atrocities of the Second World War, grew a large corpus of international humanitarian law, commencing with the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948 and expanding with the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (the ‘ICCPR’) in 1966 which provided for privacy in Article 17,
1. No one shall be subjected to arbitrary or unlawful interference with his privacy, family, home or correspondence, nor to unlawful attacks on his honour and reputation.
2. Everyone has the right to the protection of the law against such interference or attacks.


The second  major development in information privacy laws can be traced to the prospective use of computerised systems by governments to collect and process personal information in the 1960s. During this time the storage of information changed from purely paper-based records to the stored program computer. This gave rise to public concern over the threat of automated data processing. In the United States, this threat was responded to with the Privacy Act of 1974, 5 USC § 552(a) that introduced standards for the collection, storage, use and dissemination of personal information as well as introducing individual rights of access and correction on public sector agencies. In Europe, the threat was responded to with a wave of data protection laws passed in 1978 in France, Austria and Norway. The development of these national regulations gave further rise to concern over inconsistencies between national standards. In response, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development drafted Guidelines Governing the Protection of Privacy and Transborder Flows of Personal Data 1980 (the ‘OECD Guidelines’). The eight principles are summarised as,
* Collection Limitation Principle: limited, lawful and fair, with consent or knowledge;
* Data Quality Principle: relevant, accurate and up-to-date;
* Purpose Specification Principle:  detailed at the time of collection;
* Use Limitation Principle:  to specified purposes;
* Security Safeguards Principle: reasonable steps to secure;
* Openness Principle: transparency of personal data practises;
* Individual Participation Principle: including a right of access and a right of correction;
* Accountability Principle: a data controller tasked with compliance.

Every OECD nation passed similar laws domestically to govern information privacy with respect to the government agencies (except Turkey) and with respect to activities of corporations (except Turkey and US).  = Same basic standards but different levels of specificity e.g. in Australia principles based and in EU specific rules e.g. creation of data controller within each entity etc

The OECD guidelines were to influence the Australian information privacy legislation, _the Act_, but not before Australia had experienced a new threat of our own. In April 1976, the Commonwealth Government gave the ALRC a reference to study interferences with privacy arising under the laws of the Commonwealth or Commonwealth Territories. In parallel, from 1978-79, the OECD Expert Group met to prepare the OECD Guidelines.  In common to both was Michael Kirby chairman of the OECD Expert Group and Chairman of the ALRC. In 1983 the ALRC tabled its report Privacy, Report No 22 (1983) featuring two key recommendations, first, that a Privacy Act should be passed to establish information privacy principles and, second, that the Act should provide for the appointment of a Privacy Commissioner. The following year Australia acceded to the OECD Guidelines. Clarke, observes around that time, “the momentum has been lost”.  How did we regain momentum? In mid-1985 a card to prevent tax and welfare fraud was proposed. Dubbed the ‘Australia Card’, it generated national debate and protests. Its initial popularity soon declined from a high of 68% prior to the 1987 Federal election to a low of 39% by September that year. In the wake of its failure and in its place, the government sought to enhance the tax file number scheme. However, in order to gain the support of the Senate, the Privacy Bill (Cth) 1988 was (re)introduced. The Act and the Second Reading Speech made explicit reference to the ICCPR (1966), the OECD Guidelines (1980) and the ALRC, Privacy Report no 22 (1983). The Privacy Bill had the momentum it needed and Australia’s information privacy framework, the Privacy Act, passed. 

### Application
_The Act_ governs all Federal Government agencies, private sector and not-for-profit organisations with an annual turnover of more than $3 million, all private health service providers and some small businesses (collectively called ‘APP entities’) with requirements for how they must handle, use and manage personal information. There is no distinction between organisations that control or own personal information and those that provide services to owners of such information. This mean it reaches to cover outsourcing.

State government agencies governed by state equivalent legislation (e.g. in NSW its PPIPA and HRIPA).

#### Definition of Personal Information
_The Act_ applies to the handling of Personal Information, defined as
‘Information or an opinion about an identified individual, or an individual who is reasonably identifiable:
whether the information or opinion is true or not; and
whether the information or opinion is recorded in a material form or not.’

Examples:
Name, DOB, address, email address, cookies.
Pivotal to the application of the Act is whether something falls within the definition of personal information. This was considered in Grubb v Telstra.


#### Sensitive personal information
Sensitive personal information
Additional protections apply to Sensitive information
includes information or opinion about an individual’s   
* racial or ethnic origin; 
* political opinion;  
* religious beliefs; 
* sexual orientation;  
* philosophical beliefs;  
* membership of a professional or trade association or a trade union
or criminal record;  
* health information;  
* genetic information

Additional protections apply to collection, use and disclosure sensitive information. These can only be collected with consent and if the information is reasonably necessary for one or more of the organisations activities.
