# Glossary

This glossary explains legal concepts for data scientists and data science concepts for lawyers. Entries are in a single alphabetical order across both types.

Colour guide: <span class="legal">purple entries explain legal concepts for data scientists</span> and <span class="ds">orange entries explain data science concepts for lawyers</span>.

---

## A

<span class="ds">**Algorithm** — a set of step-by-step instructions a computer follows to complete a task. In law, the question is often: who is responsible when the algorithm causes harm?</span>

<span class="legal">**Authoritative source** — in legal research, a source that courts treat as binding or highly persuasive. Legislation and court judgments are authoritative; textbooks and articles are not.</span>

---

## B

<span class="ds">**Bias (algorithmic)** — when a model produces systematically skewed outputs, often because the training data reflected existing human inequalities. Courts and regulators increasingly treat this as a legal risk.</span>

---

## L

<span class="legal">**Liability** — legal responsibility for harm caused to another. In AI contexts, determining who is liable — the developer, the deployer, or the user — is often the central legal question.</span>

---

## M

<span class="ds">**Model** — a mathematical system trained on data to make predictions or decisions. When a model makes a consequential decision about a person, it may attract legal scrutiny.</span>

---

## T

<span class="ds">**Training data** — the dataset used to teach a machine learning model. The content and provenance of training data raises copyright, privacy, and consent questions in law.</span>
